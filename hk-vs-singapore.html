Hong Kong vs Singapore: Which Jurisdiction Should You Choose in 2025?

📅 January 2025 ⏱ 7 min read ✍️ Luminous Team

Hong Kong and Singapore are the two dominant financial hubs in Asia. Both offer low taxes, strong legal systems, world-class banking, and a business-friendly environment. Yet they serve different profiles of entrepreneur, and choosing the wrong one can cost you thousands in unnecessary fees — or worse, put you in the wrong tax position. This article gives you the full comparison.

Tax: the critical difference

This is where the two jurisdictions diverge most significantly. Hong Kong operates on a territorial tax system — only profits generated in Hong Kong are taxed. If your company invoices foreign clients, operates internationally, or holds assets abroad, that income is generally not taxable. The effective rate for most international entrepreneurs is zero.

Singapore taxes resident companies on income accruing in or derived from Singapore, plus foreign-sourced income when remitted to Singapore. While Singapore does offer certain foreign-source income exemptions under specific conditions, the framework is more complex and less automatic than Hong Kong's territorial approach.

For an e-commerce entrepreneur, a consultant billing European clients, or a trader dealing in goods between Asia and the West — Hong Kong's territorial system is simply more straightforward and more beneficial.

Cost: a significant gap

This is perhaps the most overlooked difference between the two jurisdictions. Setting up and maintaining a Singapore company is substantially more expensive, for several reasons:

Mandatory local director

Singapore requires at least one resident director — a Singaporean citizen, permanent resident, or holder of an Employment Pass or EntrePass. If you do not qualify, you must appoint a nominee director, which typically costs $1,500 to $3,000 per year. Hong Kong has no such requirement: a single director of any nationality, residing anywhere in the world, is sufficient.

Higher service provider fees

Due to Singapore's higher cost of living and the mandatory resident director requirement, corporate service providers charge significantly more. A basic Singapore company maintenance package rarely costs less than $2,000 per year. The equivalent in Hong Kong starts at $990 with Luminous.

GST registration

If your Singapore company exceeds SGD 1 million in taxable turnover, GST registration becomes mandatory at 9%. This adds administrative complexity and can affect pricing for certain client types. Hong Kong has no equivalent sales tax.

Banking: roughly equal, with nuances

Both jurisdictions offer excellent corporate banking, and both are home to major international banks including HSBC, Standard Chartered, DBS, and Citibank. The choice of banking jurisdiction matters less than people think — what matters is the quality of your application and the strength of your business case.

That said, Hong Kong offers a meaningful advantage for businesses with China exposure. The HK banking system has deep integration with mainland Chinese financial institutions, and CNH (offshore renminbi) accounts are far easier to maintain from Hong Kong than from Singapore.

When Singapore might be the better choice

To be balanced: Singapore is genuinely superior in certain specific situations:

  • You plan to physically relocate and live in Singapore (and want an Employment Pass)
  • Your business is primarily in Southeast Asia (Singapore is the regional hub for ASEAN)
  • You are building a crypto or Web3 business (Singapore's MAS licensing framework is more developed)
  • You are raising VC funding from institutional investors who prefer Singapore structures
  • You want access to Singapore's network of specific bilateral tax treaties

The verdict

For the vast majority of international entrepreneurs — those who run their business remotely and want a credible, tax-efficient, low-maintenance structure — Hong Kong is the better choice in 2025. It is cheaper to set up, cheaper to maintain, more tax-efficient for offshore income, and requires no local director.

Singapore is an excellent jurisdiction, but its advantages only materialise in specific contexts. Paying 2–3x more in annual fees for a jurisdiction that offers comparable banking and a more complex tax framework only makes sense if you have a specific reason to be in Singapore.
